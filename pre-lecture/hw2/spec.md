## Answers

### Q1(a) — Why "non-uniform"?

In the standard (uniform) PL condition, the coefficient relating the gradient norm to the optimality gap is a **global constant** independent of θ. Here, the coefficient

$$C(\theta) = \frac{\min_{s} \pi_\theta(a^*(s) \mid s)}{\sqrt{S} \cdot \|d_\rho^{\pi^*}/d_\mu^{\pi_\theta}\|_\infty}$$

**depends on θ** (the current policy parameters). As θ changes during training, this coefficient changes — it is non-uniform across the parameter space. When the policy is nearly optimal, $\pi_\theta(a^*(s)|s) \to 1$ and the coefficient grows; when the policy is bad, it can be very small. Hence the name *non-uniform*.

---

### Q1(b) — Origin of $\min_{s} \pi_\theta(a^*(s) \mid s)$

From the **Policy Gradient Theorem** under softmax parameterization:

$$\frac{\partial V^{\pi_\theta}(\mu)}{\partial \theta_{s,a}} = \frac{1}{1-\gamma}\, d_\mu^{\pi_\theta}(s)\, \pi_\theta(a \mid s)\, A^{\pi_\theta}(s,a)$$

In the proof of Lemma 8, the gradient norm is lower bounded by picking the optimal action coordinate for each state:

$$\left\|\frac{\partial V^{\pi_\theta}(\mu)}{\partial \theta}\right\|_2 \geq \left(\sum_s \left(\frac{\partial V^{\pi_\theta}(\mu)}{\partial \theta_{s,\,a^*(s)}}\right)^2\right)^{1/2}$$

The factor $\pi_\theta(a^*(s) \mid s)$ appears naturally from the softmax gradient formula above (evaluated at $a = a^*(s)$). To get a clean scalar lower bound, the proof takes $\min_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \pi_\theta(a^*(s) \mid s)$ to uniformly lower-bound this factor across all states.

---

### Q1(c) — Difference between $\mu$ and $\rho$

| Symbol | Role |
|--------|------|
| $\mu$ | **Training distribution** — the initial state distribution used to define the gradient and the state visitation measure $d_\mu^{\pi_\theta}$ during optimization. |
| $\rho$ | **Evaluation distribution** — the initial state distribution used to measure policy performance $V^{\pi_\theta}(\rho)$ (i.e., the objective we want to maximize). |

They can differ: we compute gradients w.r.t. trajectories starting from $\mu$, but measure convergence $V^*(\rho) - V^{\pi_\theta}(\rho)$ under $\rho$. The density ratio $d_\rho^{\pi^*}/d_\mu^{\pi_\theta}$ in the PL condition captures the mismatch between these two distributions.

### Q2(a) — Lines 100–119 in Math

**Step 1 — Critic forward pass** (Lines 101–102):

$$Q_w(s_t, \cdot) = \text{critic}(s_t), \qquad Q_w(s_t, a_t) = [Q_w(s_t,\cdot)]_{a_t}$$

**Step 2 — TD target construction** (Lines 106–112):

$$\hat{V}^{\pi_\theta}(s_{t+1}) = \sum_a \pi_\theta(a \mid s_{t+1})\, Q_w(s_{t+1}, a) \quad \text{(expected SARSA)}$$

$$y_t = \begin{cases} r_t & \text{if terminal} \\ r_t + \gamma\, \hat{V}^{\pi_\theta}(s_{t+1}) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

**Step 3 — Critic loss and update** (Lines 115–119):

$$\mathcal{L}(w) = \bigl(Q_w(s_t, a_t) - y_t\bigr)^2, \qquad w \leftarrow w - \alpha_w \nabla_w \mathcal{L}(w)$$

This is an **Expected-SARSA** style TD update: rather than bootstrapping with a single sampled next action, it computes the policy-weighted expectation $\sum_a \pi_\theta(a|s_{t+1}) Q_w(s_{t+1},a)$, which equals $V^{\pi_\theta}(s_{t+1})$ under the current critic.

### Q2(b) — Line 112: Do you agree?

**Partially disagree.**

The standard QAC critic update (as in the lecture slides) uses a **SARSA-style** single-sample target:

$$y_t = r_t + \gamma\, Q_w(s_{t+1}, a_{t+1}), \quad a_{t+1} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s_{t+1})$$

GPT instead uses the **expected** next Q-value:

$$y_t = r_t + \gamma \sum_a \pi_\theta(a \mid s_{t+1})\, Q_w(s_{t+1}, a)$$

While the expected version has **lower variance** and is a valid on-policy TD method (Expected SARSA), it deviates from the standard QAC definition. In particular, the standard QAC is on-policy — the next action $a_{t+1}$ should be sampled from $\pi_\theta$ and already available from the next environment step, so the single-sample SARSA target is both correct and computationally cheaper. GPT's design is not wrong in principle, but it is not the standard QAC algorithm.
